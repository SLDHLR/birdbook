Sri Lankan Junglefowl

Junglefowl

The Sri Lankan Junglefowl (Gallus lafayettii), Sri Lanka’s national bird, is a striking species native to the island's lowland forests and scrublands. Males are known for their vivid coloration, featuring a bright red comb, a fiery orange neck, and a deep purple tail, while females have more subdued brownish plumage, providing excellent camouflage. These birds are ground dwellers, foraging for seeds, grains, fruits, and insects in small groups or solitary pairs. Their loud, distinctive crowing is most often heard at dawn and dusk. During the breeding season, which typically occurs between February and May, males engage in territorial battles and perform elaborate courtship displays to attract females. After mating, females lay 2-4 eggs in well-hidden ground nests and take full responsibility for incubating and raising the chicks. Although the Sri Lankan Junglefowl is not endangered, deforestation and habitat loss pose long-term threats. This bird holds cultural significance in Sri Lanka and is closely related to the domestic chicken, providing insight into avian evolution. As a symbol of the country's rich biodiversity, the Junglefowl is celebrated in local traditions and artwork.


Layard's Parakeet

Layard's Parakeet

Layard's Parakeet (Psittacula calthropae), also known as the Sri Lankan Hanging Parrot, is a vibrant bird species endemic to the forests of Sri Lanka. Named after the British naturalist Edgar Leopold Layard, this parakeet is medium-sized, with a distinctive green plumage and a bluish-gray head. Males have a red patch on the shoulder and red upper mandibles, while females have a more subtle coloring. These social birds are often seen in flocks, flying rapidly through forest canopies or perching on treetops. They feed primarily on fruits, seeds, buds, and nectar, playing a vital role in forest ecosystems as seed dispersers. Layard’s Parakeets nest in tree cavities, where the female lays around 3-4 eggs. Both parents participate in raising the chicks. Though their population is stable, deforestation and habitat fragmentation pose significant threats to this species in the long term. These parakeets are beloved for their lively, chattering calls and playfulness, making them a cherished sight in Sri Lanka’s wilderness.
